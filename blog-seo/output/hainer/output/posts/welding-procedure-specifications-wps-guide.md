## What Is a Welding Procedure Specification (WPS)?

![welding procedure industrial pipeline](welding-procedure-industrial-pipeline-1.jpg)

A **Welding Procedure Specification (WPS)** is a formal written document that provides detailed instructions for making production welds. Think of it as a recipe that welders must follow to ensure consistent, code-compliant weld quality across your entire project.

For contractors and procurement managers, understanding WPS requirements isn't just about compliance—it's about **project risk management**. A properly qualified WPS protects you from costly rework, inspection failures, and potential liability issues down the line.

The WPS defines every critical variable: base metal type, filler metal specification, welding process, joint design, preheat temperature, interpass temperature, and post-weld heat treatment requirements.

## Why Does Your Project Need a Qualified WPS?

![welding inspection NDT testing](welding-inspection-ndt-testing-1.jpg)

Wondering whether you can skip the WPS qualification for smaller jobs? That's a common question, but the answer depends on your project's governing codes.

**ASME Section IX** and **AWS D1.1** both mandate WPS qualification for most pressure-containing applications. Here's what happens without a qualified WPS:

| Risk Factor | Potential Impact |
|------------|------------------|
| **Code Non-Compliance** | Failed inspections, project delays, contractual penalties |
| **Quality Inconsistency** | Variable weld properties, unpredictable performance |
| **Liability Exposure** | Increased risk in case of in-service failures |
| **Client Confidence** | Reduced trust from owners and engineering firms |

For oil & gas, petrochemical, and power generation projects, WPS qualification isn't optional—it's a **contractual requirement**.

## The WPS-PQR-WPQ Relationship

![welding procedure industrial pipeline](welding-procedure-industrial-pipeline-2.jpg)

Understanding how these three documents work together is essential for managing your welding program effectively.

### WPS (Welding Procedure Specification)

The WPS is the **instruction document** given to welders. It specifies:
- Base material specifications (ASTM grades, P-Numbers)
- Filler metal classification (AWS A5.x specifications)
- Welding process (SMAW, GTAW, GMAW, FCAW, SAW)
- Joint geometry and preparation
- Electrical characteristics (polarity, amperage range, voltage range)
- Travel speed parameters
- Shielding gas composition and flow rate
- Preheat and interpass temperature ranges
- Post-weld heat treatment (PWHT) requirements

### PQR (Procedure Qualification Record)

The PQR is the **test report** that proves your WPS produces sound welds. It includes:
- Actual welding parameters used during testing
- Base metal and filler metal certifications
- Mechanical test results (tensile, bend, impact, hardness)
- NDE reports (RT, UT, MT, PT)
- Date of qualification and testing laboratory information

**Key point**: The PQR supports the WPS. You cannot have a qualified WPS without a supporting PQR.

### WPQ (Welder Performance Qualification)

While the WPS qualifies the *procedure*, the WPQ qualifies the *welder*. Even with a perfect WPS, only welders with valid WPQs for that specific WPS can perform production welding.

## Essential Variables vs. Non-Essential Variables

Not all changes to your WPS require requalification. ASME Section IX divides variables into three categories:

### Essential Variables

These **require requalification** if changed. Examples include:
- Change in base metal P-Number group
- Change in filler metal F-Number
- Addition or deletion of shielding gas
- Significant change in heat input
- Change from single-pass to multi-pass technique
- Deletion of preheat or PWHT

### Non-Essential Variables

These can be modified **without requalification**. Examples:
- Joint design details (within qualified limits)
- Welding position (for certain processes)
- Travel speed (within qualified range)
- Specific amperage (as long as within qualified range)

### Supplementary Essential Variables

These become essential only when **impact testing** is required:
- Heat input limitations
- Specific temper bead requirements
- Specific cooling rate controls

## Common WPS Qualification Tests

![welding inspection NDT testing](welding-inspection-ndt-testing-2.jpg)

Your PQR will include mechanical testing to verify weld integrity. The specific tests depend on your application code:

### Tensile Testing

Verifies the weld's ultimate tensile strength meets or exceeds the base metal's minimum specified strength. Typically requires two tensile specimens for butt welds.

### Bend Testing

Demonstrates weld ductility and soundness. Root bend and face bend specimens are bent 180° around a specified mandrel without cracking.

### Impact Testing (Charpy V-Notch)

Required for low-temperature service applications. Tests the weld's toughness at specified service temperatures.

### Hardness Testing

Verifies the weld and heat-affected zone (HAZ) don't exceed maximum hardness limits, particularly important for sour service applications (NACE MR0175).

### Macro/Micro Examination

Cross-sectional analysis to verify weld penetration, fusion, and microstructure.

## WPS Format and Required Content

![welding procedure industrial pipeline](welding-procedure-industrial-pipeline-3.jpg)

A properly formatted WPS should include these sections:

```
WPS No.: _______________    Date: _______________
Revision: _______________   PQR No.: _______________

1. JOINT CONFIGURATION
   - Joint type (butt, fillet, socket, etc.)
   - Backing material (if used)
   - Weld progression (single-pass, multi-pass)

2. BASE METALS
   - P-Number: ______  Group Number: ______
   - Material specification (ASTM A106 Gr.B, etc.)
   - Thickness range qualified

3. FILLER METALS
   - F-Number: ______  A-Number: ______
   - AWS classification (E7018, ER70S-6, etc.)
   - Diameter range: ______
   - Supplemental filler metal (if applicable)

4. WELDING PROCESS
   - Process type (SMAW, GTAW, GMAW, etc.)
   - Current type and polarity (DCEP, DCEN, AC)

5. ELECTRICAL CHARACTERISTICS
   - Amperage range: ______
   - Voltage range: ______
   - Travel speed range: ______

6. SHIELDING GAS
   - Composition: ______
   - Flow rate: ______

7. THERMAL TREATMENT
   - Preheat temperature: ______
   - Interpass temperature: ______
   - PWHT temperature and time: ______

8. TECHNIQUE
   - String or weave bead
   - Initial and interpass cleaning methods
   - Peening requirements (if applicable)
```

## Material Groupings: P-Numbers and F-Numbers

ASME Section IX uses grouping systems to reduce the number of qualifications required.

### P-Numbers (Base Metals)

Base metals are grouped by weldability characteristics:

| P-Number | Material Type | Examples |
|----------|--------------|----------|
| P-1 | Carbon Steel | ASTM A106, A516, A234 WPB |
| P-3 | Low Alloy Steel | ASTM A335 P11, P22 |
| P-4 | Alloy Steel | ASTM A335 P5, P9 |
| P-5 | Chromium-Molybdenum | 5Cr-½Mo, 9Cr-1Mo |
| P-6 | Martensitic Stainless | 410, 420 |
| P-7 | Ferritic Stainless | 405, 430 |
| P-8 | Austenitic Stainless | 304, 316, 321 |
| P-9 | Nickel Alloys | Inconel 625, Hastelloy C276 |
| P-10 | Titanium & Zirconium | Gr.1, Gr.2 Titanium |

**Qualification rule**: Qualifying with a higher P-Number typically qualifies lower P-Numbers within the same group.

### F-Numbers (Filler Metals)

Filler metals are grouped by usability characteristics:

| F-Number | Description | Examples |
|----------|-------------|----------|
| F-1 | Carbon Steel Electrodes | E6010, E7018 |
| F-2 | Low Alloy Steel Electrodes | E8018-B2, E9018-B3 |
| F-3 | Stainless Steel Electrodes | E308-16, E316-15 |
| F-4 | Nickel Alloy Electrodes | ENiCrFe-3, ENiCrMo-3 |
| F-5 | Copper Alloy Electrodes | ECu, ECuSi |
| F-6 | Aluminum Alloy Electrodes | E4043, E5356 |

## Common WPS Mistakes to Avoid

Based on our experience reviewing contractor documentation, here are the most frequent errors we see:

### ❌ Incomplete Variable Documentation

Listing amperage as "150A" instead of a qualified range like "140-180A". The WPS should specify ranges that were actually tested and qualified.

### ❌ Missing Essential Variables

Forgetting to specify shielding gas composition, back purging requirements, or temper bead techniques when these were used during qualification.

### ❌ Thickness Range Errors

Qualifying with ½" coupon but claiming qualification for ¼" to 2" production thickness. ASME Section IX has specific rules about qualified thickness ranges based on coupon thickness.

### ❌ PQR/WPS Mismatch

The PQR shows actual amperage of 165A, but the WPS specifies 120-140A. The WPS range must encompass the actual values recorded on the PQR.

### ❌ Expired Qualifications

Some codes require WPS requalification after significant code edition changes or if the procedure hasn't been used for extended periods.

## Industry-Specific WPS Requirements

![welding procedure industrial pipeline](welding-procedure-industrial-pipeline-4.jpg)

Different industries have additional WPS considerations beyond ASME Section IX:

### Oil & Gas (ASME B31.3 / B31.4 / B31.8)

- Sour service applications require hardness testing per NACE MR0175/ISO 15156
- Additional impact testing for low-temperature pipelines
- Strict controls on heat input for certain materials

### Power Generation (ASME Section I / VIII)

- Enhanced documentation requirements for nuclear applications (ASME Section III)
- Specific WPS requirements for boiler tube welding
- Additional bend test specimens for certain applications

### Structural Steel (AWS D1.1)

- Different P-Number grouping system
- Prequalified WPS options for common carbon steel joints
- Specific requirements for cyclic loading applications

### Shipbuilding (ABS / DNV / Lloyd's)

- Classification society approval required
- Additional testing for cryogenic service
- Specific requirements for aluminum-to-steel transitions

## Managing Your WPS Library

For contractors running multiple projects, maintaining an organized WPS library is critical:

### Digital Organization

- Use consistent naming conventions (WPS-XXX-SMAW-CS-001)
- Link each WPS to its supporting PQR(s) in your database
- Track revision history and superseded procedures
- Maintain backup copies off-site

### Qualification Tracking

- Monitor expiration dates for time-limited qualifications
- Track which WPSs are active on which projects
- Maintain welder qualification matrices linked to WPS library

### Continuous Improvement

- Review WPS performance data from production welding
- Update procedures based on field feedback
- Consolidate similar WPSs to reduce library complexity

## When to Develop New WPS vs. Modify Existing

Not every job requires a brand-new WPS qualification. Consider these scenarios:

### Modify Existing WPS When:
- Base metal change is within the same P-Number group
- Only non-essential variables are changing
- Thickness range is within already qualified limits
- Filler metal change is within the same F-Number

### Develop New WPS When:
- Base metal P-Number changes to a different group
- New welding process is required
- Application requires impact testing and existing WPS wasn't impact qualified
- Client specifications exceed your current qualification limits

## Frequently Asked Questions

### Can I use a WPS from another company?

No. WPS qualifications are **company-specific**. A WPS qualified by Contractor A cannot be used by Contractor B, even if the procedures are identical. Each contractor must qualify their own WPSs.

### How long does a WPS remain valid?

ASME Section IX doesn't specify an expiration date. However, WPSs may become invalid if:
- The governing code edition changes and the WPS doesn't meet new requirements
- The procedure isn't used for an extended period (company policy dependent)
- Essential variables are modified without requalification

### Do I need separate WPSs for each material thickness?

Not necessarily. A single WPS can qualify a **thickness range** based on the test coupon thickness. For example, qualifying with a 1" coupon typically covers ¼" to 2" production thickness.

### Can one PQR support multiple WPSs?

Yes. A single PQR can support multiple WPSs as long as all essential variables are documented and each WPS falls within the qualified ranges established by the PQR.

### What's the difference between prequalified and qualified WPS?

**Prequalified WPS** (AWS D1.1 only) are procedures that don't require testing if they meet specific code-specified parameters. **Qualified WPS** requires actual testing and PQR documentation. ASME Section IX doesn't recognize prequalified WPS—all procedures must be qualified.
